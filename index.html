Deel · 2025

Elevate Documents Experience

Company
Deel
My Role
Product Designer
Topic
Product Design
Year
2025
Outcome

We redesigned Deel's document creation experience from scratch. The old flow was manual, fragmented and slow. The new one is AI-assisted: it generates content and prefills data from the client's company profile, so HR teams spend their time reviewing instead of typing. Document creation time dropped by over 60%, errors at the point of sending fell sharply, and documents went from being a pain point to one of the most used features on the platform.

−64%
Reduction in average time to create and send a document, from first click to recipient delivery
3×
Increase in document template adoption after the editor redesign simplified the creation flow
−41%
Drop in incomplete or incorrectly filled documents, driven by AI prefill from company and contractor data
The Challenge
Documents were an afterthought in a platform built on them

At Deel, documents are foundational. Contracts, NDAs, amendments and compliance files sit at the centre of every hiring and payment flow. But the experience had grown organically over years, with multiple entry points, inconsistent states, and no clear ownership of actions.


HR teams were spending an average of 18 minutes manually filling a single document, copying company data they already had elsewhere in Deel, writing boilerplate clauses from scratch, and hunting for the right template across a disorganised library. Contractors had no visibility into what was pending or why. Documents needed to become a real product, not a backlog item.

The Editor
A document editor built for speed and confidence

The core of the redesign was a completely new document editor. Rather than starting from a blank canvas, users land in a guided creation environment with structure already in place and intelligence built into every field.


We mapped every document state (draft, pending signature, signed, expired, voided) and gave each a clear, consistent visual language. The creation flow went from a multi-step wizard with ambiguous entry points to a single linear experience: choose type, review prefilled data, customise, send.


Bulk actions, template management and signing flows were consolidated into one unified system that behaved the same way regardless of document type or legal jurisdiction.

AI Integration
AI that fills the gaps before the user has to

The most impactful decision in the project was integrating AI content generation directly into the editor. When a user starts a new document, the system pulls from the client's company profile (legal name, registered address, jurisdiction, active contracts, contractor details) and prefills all standard fields automatically.


Beyond data prefill, the AI can generate document body content: standard clauses, role-specific language, jurisdiction-appropriate legal wording. These surface as editable suggestions rather than locked outputs. The user stays in control; the AI just removes the blank-page problem.


Designing this required a lot of thinking about trust. We iterated on how to signal what the AI had generated versus what the user had confirmed, and how to make it easy to override without interrupting the flow. The final pattern used subtle inline attribution with a one-click edit model that felt transparent without getting in the way.

The System
States, permissions, and scale

Alongside the editor, we redesigned the full document hub where HR managers track everything across their organisation. The old version had no filtering, no bulk management, and no clear way to see who was blocking a flow.


The new hub was built around a status-first architecture: every document shows its current state, the next required action, and who owns it. Overdue items get flagged automatically. Bulk sending, reminders and archiving were added as proper first-class actions, built for teams managing documents across dozens of jurisdictions at the same time.

What I did
  • Led end-to-end design for the document editor redesign, from the initial experience audit and user research through to final handoff specs and QA
  • Defined the information architecture for all document states and the hub view, making pending actions and ownership immediately readable for both senders and recipients
  • Designed the AI-assisted creation flow: how prefilled fields are presented, how generated content is attributed and edited, and how to build trust in AI suggestions without adding friction
  • Worked closely with the AI/ML team to translate model capabilities into interaction patterns, making sure what we designed was technically feasible and reflected the actual confidence level of the model outputs
  • Collaborated with legal and compliance stakeholders to make sure the editor handled the full range of contract types, jurisdictions and signatory configurations Deel operates across
  • Ran usability testing with HR managers across three markets to validate the new flows, identified friction points around AI-generated content and iterated until trust signals felt natural rather than interruptive
  • Built and maintained the Figma component extensions required for the document system, keeping everything consistent with Deel's broader design system
What I learned

Designing AI features taught me that the hardest UX problem is not capability, it's trust. The AI could prefill accurately 85% of the time, but if users couldn't tell what was prefilled versus what they had entered themselves, they would either over-trust it and send wrong documents, or ignore it entirely and lose the speed benefit. The attribution and edit model we landed on took more iteration than any other part of the project.

Working in a compliance-heavy context forced a level of precision that most product design doesn't require. Every label, every state, every generated clause had legal implications. The instinct to simplify had to be balanced against the need for accuracy. I came away with a much sharper sense of how to make something feel simple without hiding complexity that users actually need to see.

Building a design team

Company
Casavo
My Role
Product Design Lead
Duration

Year

Outcome

Casavo went from having no formal design practice to a fully operational team with clear roles, shared rituals, and a unified knowledge base — all within six months. Design decisions became visible, traceable, and trusted across the organisation.

2×
Increase in design output velocity after standardising file and delivery structure
3
Linked Notion databases built to connect research, users, and product insights
3
Weekly design ceremonies introduced with concrete goals and cross-team participation
The Challenge
Building from zero, with no playbook

When I joined Casavo, design barely existed. There were no defined roles, no shared rituals, and no agreed-upon deliverables. Engineering and Strategy had little visibility into what design was doing — or why.

The first challenge wasn’t designing screens. It was designing the team itself

The Work
Ceremonies, artifacts, and a single source of truth

We began by setting up a structured approach to our work, built around three core pillars: Responsibilities, Ceremonies, and Artifacts.

First, we clarified the responsibilities and boundaries of each role, creating a shared understanding of ownership across the team.


We then defined clear goals for our ceremonies, turning them into meaningful moments for sharing progress, aligning, and continuously improving the way we work.


Finally, we worked closely with Strategy and Engineering to better understand their needs from our design files. This led us to design a new structure and improve the overall organization of our deliverables, making them more accessible and effective for cross-team collaboration

Artifact
Notion as north star

We chose Notion as a single source of truth to document all our practices and decisions, and to use it as a repository for our data.

Specifically, for discovery activities, we defined three interconnected databases:

  • Research Projects: which map each discovery activity, including the owner and date.
  • Users: which contains all our users, along with their contact details and brief feedback.
  • Insights: which includes a synthesis of the feedback gathered from research. We use it to align with Strategy and plan the next design activities

What I did
  • Defined role boundaries for Product Designer and Content Designer, removing ambiguity that had caused duplicated effort and missed handoffs
  • Designed and facilitated four recurring ceremonies, setting explicit success criteria for each so they never became empty rituals
  • Negotiated a unified file and delivery structure with engineering and strategy leads, reducing rework at handoff by standardising what "done" looks like

  • Built three linked Notion databases: Research Projects and Users, Insights making the team's knowledge navigable and useful for planning

  • Ran the first cross-functional UX Review, establishing it as a recurring space where design decisions were explained and challenged openly
What I learned

The hardest part of building a design team isn’t design, it’s the politics.

Getting engineers and strategists to trust a new process requires consistency: showing up, again and again, before asking for anything in return.

The ceremonies only worked because I ran them rigorously, even when attendance was low.

I also learned that documentation is a form of design. The Notion system only became truly useful when I treated it like a product, with clear information architecture, defined ownership, and continuous feedback loops.

A repository no one uses is just organized clutter

Outcome

Treatwell shipped a stable, three-tier design system — covering foundations, B2B, and B2C — mirrored in both Figma and Storybook. Design velocity increased, cross-team inconsistencies dropped, and the system became something every designer and engineer could contribute to without breaking.

The Challenge
One company, two platforms, zero consistency

Treatwell runs a B2B product for salon professionals and a B2C platform for customers. Both had grown independently, developing overlapping but misaligned component libraries. The cost was real: duplicated work, visual inconsistencies, and a codebase where changing one thing broke another. The system needed to be rebuilt from the ground up, not patched.

Design system structure
Architecture first, components second

We proposed a three-tier structure: a shared foundation layer (Aphrodite) containing tokens, typography, and colour; a B2B UI library (Armonia) for the salon management product; and a B2C platform library (Eros) for the consumer app. Every layer was mirrored in Figma and Storybook. Weekly co-creation sessions shaped the roadmap, while bi-weekly shadowing in real salons validated components in the wild.

Testing
Testing directly with real users

We tested our components directly with users through shadowing and usability testing sessions.

Shadowing sessions were conducted in the field with our users, typically every two to three weeks.

We combined these qualitative insights with quantitative data from Google Analytics to inform the next iterations and improvements.

What I did
  • Audited the existing Figma libraries and Storybook to map duplications and identify where the two platforms diverged most critically
  • Proposed and defined the three-tier architecture with the core team, getting buy-in from both design and engineering before building anything
  • Led weekly roadmap sessions, balancing component creation with maintenance governance and contribution guidelines

  • Designed and ran bi-weekly shadowing sessions with salon professionals, sitting with users in their working environment to observe how components performed under real conditions

  • Wrote the contribution and issue-reporting process that allowed anyone in the company to participate without degrading quality
What I learned

A design system only works if engineers trust it as much as designers do. Mirroring Figma and Storybook from the start, rather than building one and retrofitting the other, was the single most important architectural decision we made. It removed the excuse not to use it.

I also learned that in-context testing is irreplaceable. Reviewing components in Figma told us what they looked like. Watching a salon professional use them on a busy Saturday morning told us whether they actually worked. There is no substitute for that gap.

The Challenge
Banking that means something

Flowe launched as one of the first Italian mobile banks to connect financial behavior with lifestyle and sustainability.

The app gave users a single place to manage their money, track their wellbeing, and build positive habits, all brought together through a dynamic homepage that changed shape based on how they were performing across four key dimensions.

The Work
The homepage as a mirror

The centrepiece was a dynamic visual bubble on the homepage. An organic shape that literally reflected the user's balance across the four impact areas. It shifted based on real data: savings progress, Virtuoso fitness activity, courses completed, trees planted via Treedom. It wasn't decorative. It was a status signal that told you, at a glance, who you were being that week.

What I did
  • Defined the UX epics across all banking and lifestyle features at the start of the project, creating a shared map for the Agile team to work from
  • Designed the core flows for financial awareness — savings goals, spending visualisation, and income tracking — from kick-off through to testing and rework
  • Designed the homepage dynamic visual concept: how the shape would behave, what data would drive it, and how to communicate its meaning to a first-time user
  • Collaborated on the integration model with Treedom and Virtuoso, translating third-party data into in-app moments users could actually feel
  • Contributed to the design system alongside the UI team, ensuring the visual language held together from core banking flows through to sustainability features
What I learned

The most challenging part wasn't designing complex flows — it was designing meaning. The dynamic homepage visual looked simple in the final product, but behind it was a lot of work on what the shape should communicate, how quickly it should change, and how to avoid making users feel judged rather than supported.

Working in Agile at this scale also taught me that the kick-off phase is where the most important design decisions happen. The epics we defined in the first two weeks shaped everything that came after. Getting that structure right — before anyone had opened Figma — was the most valuable contribution I made to this project.

Sky Wi-Fi launched with a mapped, validated end-to-end customer journey covering more than 100 service capabilities. Every product owner had a clear, designed reference point for their area — and the gaps that would have caused costly rework in production were caught and resolved in design.

The challenge
Designing a service at scale, with five organisations in the room

Sky was launching a new broadband service — and needed to design not just screens, but every touchpoint a customer would encounter, from the first ad to the technician visit to the cancellation call. More than 100 distinct capabilities. Five organisations at the table. Fourteen months. The risk wasn't that we'd design something bad. It was that the pieces wouldn't connect.

The work
Designing the experience framework

Sketchin's role was to map every phase of the experience and make it actionable for product owners across all workstreams.

This involved close collaboration with subject matter experts, translating domain knowledge into clear user flows, and shaping a shared foundation to guide product decisions.

the work
Managing Complexity and preventing rework

The fragmented and asynchronous nature of the project introduced significant complexity, often resulting in rework across the design process.

To address this, we systematically reviewed mockups to ensure consistency and usability, identifying gaps and edge cases early, before they could turn into product issues.

What I did
  • Mapped the as-is customer journey across all major service touchpoints, identifying gaps and uncovered scenarios that no one had formally documented
  • Co-created detailed experience flows with Sky SMEs, translating domain-specific knowledge into designed screens product owners could use as a working reference
  • Reviewed all designed mockups for consistency and usability across the full service scope — flagging missing edge cases and misalignments before they reached development
  • Coordinated with analysts from EY and Bip to ensure the experience design reflected the operational realities of the service — not just the ideal-state scenario
  • Maintained design quality across a project that was intentionally fragmented by workstream, ensuring the customer experience read as a single coherent journey end-to-end
What I learned

Scale changes the nature of design work. On a project with 100+ capabilities and five organisations, the most important design skill is synthesis — knowing how to take what SMEs know, what analysts have measured, and what engineers can build, and turn it into something a product owner can act on.

I also learned that QA is not the end of design — it's a continuation of it. Every inconsistency we caught in the review phase was a design problem that had been deferred, not avoided. The discipline to hold quality across a fragmented project is its own kind of craft.

My Ferrari Mobile

Client
Ferrari
My Role
Product Designer

Mobile App

Owner Experience
Outcome

Ferrari owners received a mobile experience that felt native to the brand — personal, precise, and built around the moments that matter most to someone who owns one of the world's most iconic cars. The app connected drivers to their vehicle history, service, and the Ferrari community in a single, crafted interface.

1
Unified app replacing fragmented owner touchpoints across web, paper, and phone
High
User satisfaction among one of the world's most demanding and brand-aware audiences
0
Compromise on brand standards — every detail reviewed against Ferrari's expectation of perfection
The Challenge
Designing for the most demanding users in the world

Ferrari owners don't just own a car — they have a relationship with a legend. The brief was to build an app worthy of that relationship: one that gave owners access to their car's history, upcoming service, exclusive events, and the broader Ferrari world. The challenge wasn't features. It was standard. Everything had to feel like Ferrari — or it wasn't good enough.

The Work
Craft at every level of the stack

We began with deep brand immersion — understanding not just Ferrari's visual identity but the emotional register of the brand and how owners experience it. Owner research revealed that the relationship is deeply personal and pride-based. Every screen was designed to honour that: clear hierarchy, restrained motion, typography treated as a precision instrument, and not a single element that felt generic or off-brand.

What I did
  • Led the owner research phase — interviewing Ferrari owners to understand the moments, emotions, and information they most valued in their relationship with the brand
  • Designed the core owner flows: vehicle history, service scheduling, event access, and the personal connection features that distinguished this from a generic car app
  • Developed the interaction model — defining how transitions, gestures, and states should behave to feel premium without being theatrical
  • Reviewed every design decision against Ferrari's brand standards, working closely with the client team to ensure nothing slipped through that felt generic or misaligned
  • Delivered a comprehensive design specification that gave the engineering team the detail they needed to build to the same level of finish we had designed to
What I learned

Working with Ferrari taught me that luxury is not a style — it's a standard of attention. The details that most products treat as edge cases are, for Ferrari, the main event. A loading state, a transition timing, a label that's two words instead of one — these things matter enormously when the user is someone who chose this brand precisely because it doesn't cut corners.

I also came away with a much stronger sense of how brand fluency changes the nature of design work. The more deeply you understand what a brand stands for and why, the more confident you can be in every decision — and the faster you can move, because you're not second-guessing whether something feels right.
